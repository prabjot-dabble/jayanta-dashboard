Campaign diagnostic · 61 campaigns · 8 weeks · ₹1,54,310 spend

The spend spike was three campaigns, and two of them broke.

Portfolio ROAS fell from 4.64 in W24 to 2.74 in W28 — a 41% collapse. It was not gradual and not broad. In W28 alone, spend rose ₹4,478 while sales fell ₹2,734: marginal ROAS of −0.61. Three campaigns absorbed ₹5,158 of the increase. One of them, SPM finger Paints KW/Broad Evits, took ₹3,764 at ROAS 1.14 and a ₹21.88 CPC. Meanwhile 22 of 61 campaigns spent nothing at all for eight straight weeks.

01 Portfolio summary

What this file adds. The previous workbook gave four weeks of ASIN-level totals. This gives eight weeks × 61 campaigns, which closes the largest gap in the earlier analysis: campaign type splits, per-campaign efficiency, and the ability to attribute the W27–W28 deterioration to specific line items rather than to the account as a whole. It does not contain keywords, search terms, placements, budgets or impression share — those gaps remain.

02 Reconciliation with the ASIN file — read this before using either number

The two uploads overlap on weeks 25–28 but do not agree. This is a material finding, not a rounding issue.

WeekAll-campaign spend ₹ASIN-file spend ₹Spend ratioAll-campaign sales ₹ASIN-file sales ₹Sales ratio
ASIN-attributed sales exceed total campaign sales in all four weeks — by 21%, 5%, 25% and 35%. A single ASIN cannot generate more ad sales than every campaign in the category combined. And spend flips direction: the ASIN file is below campaign spend in W25–W26, then above it in W27–W28.

The most likely explanation is different attribution windows or an ASIN-level report that includes halo and brand-halo sales while the campaign report shows advertised-ASIN sales only. Practical consequence: the ROAS 5.32→3.52 figure from the earlier dashboard and the 4.64→2.74 figure here are not the same measurement. This dashboard uses the campaign file exclusively, since it is internally consistent across all 61 campaigns and 8 weeks. Confirm the attribution setting with your ads team before quoting either figure externally.

06 The W28 event · where the money went

Week 27 to Week 28: spend +₹4,478, sales −₹2,734. Marginal ROAS −0.61. This table attributes the change campaign by campaign.

CampaignΔ Spend ₹Δ Sales ₹Marginal ROASΔ ImpressionsW28 CPC ₹W28 ROASAssessment
Three campaigns took 92% of the increase, and they split cleanly.

Working: SPM|Braod|HighIS|FingerPaint\EV (+₹2,287 → marginal ROAS 2.9) and SPM|FingerPaint|NewKW-1|EV (+₹2,157 → marginal ROAS 3.5). Both scaled and both returned above 2.5. These are not the problem.

Broken: SPM finger Paints KW/Broad Evits took ₹460 more and lost ₹6,094 of sales — marginal ROAS −13.2. Its W28 CPC hit ₹21.88, the highest of any campaign at scale, on ROAS 1.14. This single campaign spent ₹3,764 in W28 to return ₹4,297, and it has been deteriorating for four weeks: ROAS 4.19 in W21–24, 2.56 in W25–28.

Also broken: SP_CT_Starter_fingerpaints_painting_EV raised spend ₹386 to produce zero sales in W28, and SPT|Finger Paints|Comp|EV spent ₹848 for one order (ROAS 0.55).

07 Waste and dormancy

CampaignZero-sale spend ₹Weeks with spend, no salesTotal spend ₹Overall ROAS
₹21,732 — 14.1% of all spend — went to campaign-weeks that returned either nothing or an ACOS above 50%. Of that, ₹7,518 produced literally zero sales across 85 campaign-weeks.

Separately, 22 of 61 campaigns spent ₹0 across all eight weeks, and 18 of those generated zero impressions. That is 36% of the account structure sitting inert — not costing money, but adding management overhead and, more importantly, suggesting the account has been built up over time without pruning. Several dormant names duplicate active ones (SPM|FingerPaints|EV, SPM|FingerPaint|NewKW-3|EV, SPT|FingerPaint|BSR-2|EV), which is a common source of accidental internal bid competition when they are reactivated.
